When commission spreadsheets fail everyone

Every time a relationship manager closes a loan at an Indian bank, someone manually calculates their incentive. That someone is usually a junior analyst with a spreadsheet, the number is almost never right on the first try, and the RM waits days to find out what they earned. At Kobl, I worked as a PM intern to build the system that makes this automatic, transparent, and configurable by any branch admin — no code required.

01

The Problem

Indian banks manage incentives for thousands of relationship managers across hundreds of branches. The incentive logic isn't simple — it varies by product type, employee designation, region, and active campaigns. A rural loan RM earns differently than an urban investments RM. A Silver-tier rep in Maharashtra earns differently than a Gold-tier rep in Karnataka. And both of those change the moment a new seasonal scheme goes live.

The way most banks handled this in practice: a junior analyst at each branch maintained a spreadsheet. Every deal closure triggered a manual lookup — check the tier, apply the regional modifier, account for cross-sell bonuses, subtract clawbacks — then send the number to payroll three to five days later. If the RM thought the number was wrong, they filed a dispute. Most of the time, they were at least partially right.

The downstream effects were predictable: errors, disputes, delayed payouts, and RMs who simply stopped trusting the numbers. High performers — the ones you most need to retain — were most affected, because they had the most at stake. The incentive system designed to motivate people was actively demotivating them.

02

Who I Designed For

The product had three distinct users with distinct needs — and they were in tension with each other. Branch admins needed flexibility. RMs needed transparency. Compliance needed airtight audit trails. Designing for one without the others would have solved part of the problem and introduced new ones.

B
Branch Admin
Non-technical · configures rules for their branch
Goals
  • Change incentive rules instantly without IT
  • Run time-bound campaigns for new products
  • See how rule changes will affect payouts before saving
Frustrations
  • Every rule change requires an IT ticket and a 2-week wait
  • No preview mode — changes go live immediately
R
Relationship Manager
Sales rep · receives and disputes incentives
Goals
  • See exactly how their payout was calculated
  • Track earnings in real time as deals close
  • Resolve disputes without a multi-day back-and-forth
Frustrations
  • Payouts arrive as a single number, no breakdown
  • No way to verify without asking a manager
C
Compliance Officer
Finance / audit · needs RBI-ready records
Goals
  • Full audit trail for every incentive calculation
  • Rule versioning so historical calculations can be reconstructed
  • One-click RBI-format export for audits
Frustrations
  • Spreadsheet history is unreliable and easily overwritten
  • No version control on past rule logic
03

Decisions That Shaped the Product

The architecture of this product came down to four PM decisions. Each involved a real tradeoff — not obvious in either direction. These were the ones I spent the most time on.

01
Store rules as JSON logic, not hardcoded per branch
The tempting path was to build each branch's rules directly into the database schema — fast to ship, simple to compute. We chose JSON logic evaluated at runtime instead. The upfront engineering cost was higher, but it meant any admin could define AND/OR conditional logic through the UI without a developer touching anything. This single decision unlocked the whole no-code promise. Without it, every branch update would still need IT.
Core architecture decision
02
Real-time calculation vs. end-of-month batch
Every existing incentive system runs on a monthly batch job. It's simpler and cheaper to compute. We went real-time instead — recalculating the moment a deal closes, a status updates, or a rule changes retroactively. The reason: the core RM trust problem couldn't be solved by a number that appeared once a month. RMs needed to see their earnings move when they closed a deal. Real-time calculation turned incentives from a payout into feedback.
UX-driven technical choice
03
Four-level hierarchy: Branch → Region → Zone → National
Some incentive modifiers apply nationally (new product launch), some regionally (zone performance bonuses), some at branch level. We built a four-tier override model so each level could add its own logic without removing what's inherited from above. A branch admin can add a local modifier; they can't remove a national compliance rule. This preserved autonomy without creating compliance gaps — a distinction that came directly from our compliance officer interviews.
Governance model
04
Audit trail as a core feature, not a log
Most systems log transactions as an afterthought — a timestamped record that compliance teams dig through when something goes wrong. We built the audit trail as a first-class product surface: every RM can see their complete incentive trail, every rule has a version history, and the dashboard exports directly in RBI audit format. The business case was compliance. The product case was trust. Both needed the same feature.
Compliance + trust driver

05

Impact

The product shipped to multiple bank branches and immediately changed how incentive management worked at those institutions. The 60% reduction in disputes wasn't just an efficiency metric — it was a signal that RMs finally trusted their numbers. When people stop disputing payouts, it means the transparency is working.

The 70% reduction in computation time closed the gap that was hurting morale most. Moving from a 3–5 day wait to real-time calculation made incentives feel like feedback instead of a delayed report card. That psychological shift — closing a deal and immediately seeing it reflected in your earnings — was the part I was most invested in getting right.

The compliance angle surprised me most. I came in thinking the audit trail was a regulatory checkbox. What I learned was that it was actually the feature RMs wanted most — not for compliance, but because it let them show a manager exactly where they thought their payout was wrong. Transparency and auditability turned out to be the same feature solving two different user problems.

70% Faster incentive computation vs. manual spreadsheet

What I'd measure next

North star metric: RM dispute rate per 100 deals closed. Alongside that: time-to-payout from deal closure, and rule update frequency by branch (a proxy for how much autonomy is actually being used). The 60% dispute reduction is the headline — but dispute rate per deal, not total disputes, is what scales cleanly across branches of different sizes. I'd also want to track whether the Compliance Issues Flagged count trends down over time as admins learn the system, or stays flat — which would indicate a training problem, not a product problem.

06

What I Learned

01
Transparency and auditability are the same feature
I designed the audit trail for compliance officers. But when I showed RMs the full breakdown view, they were the ones who responded the most. The feature that satisfies a regulatory requirement is often the same feature that builds end-user trust — you just have to surface it to both audiences.
02
Real-time vs. batch isn't an engineering choice — it's a product one
The recommendation from the engineering team was batch processing. It's simpler, cheaper, and standard practice. I pushed back because I understood that the RM trust problem couldn't be solved by a number that appeared once a month. Changing the cadence of feedback changed how people felt about the product entirely.
03
The people who adapt are telling you something important
Branch admins had built workarounds — shadow spreadsheets, Slack messages to IT, informal region-level agreements — to manage the flexibility the official system didn't have. Every workaround was a feature request that nobody filed. Mapping those workarounds was more valuable than the formal requirements list.
04
Scope ruthlessly at the architecture level, not just the feature level
The JSON logic rule engine was a bigger upfront bet than a hardcoded schema. It was the right call — but it required defending at every design review. Scope decisions at the architecture level have downstream consequences for everything built on top. That's where PM input matters most, and it's usually the conversation that happens without you.

"The incentive system designed to motivate people was actively demotivating them — and the fix was just making the math visible."

This project taught me that product problems in enterprise software almost always come back to trust. The features people ask for on the surface — speed, accuracy, flexibility — are often proxies for a single deeper need: confidence that the system is working in their interest.
