How I got here
It started with a Nintendo DS.

I wasn't hooked on the games so much as the hardware: how the dual screens and the touchscreen were built so a kid could just pick it up and know what to do. That was my first proof that a great product takes both engineering sophistication and real design choices, and it's what pointed me toward computer engineering.

The turn toward product came from a failure. On a course project building ML-based route optimization, three days before the demo we tried to connect the modules we'd each built, and they couldn't talk to each other. Every piece worked on its own; no one had designed the system as a whole. That was the lesson: strong parts aren't enough without coordinated decisions around a shared product vision.

It's been the same thread ever since. I don't think knowing how to build something counts for much if the reasoning behind it doesn't hold up. That's why I'm headed to Dartmouth's MEM, and why I want to spend my career in product: where the technical meets the human.

People & teams
I read team dynamics the way some people read balance sheets.

I grew up moving four times across India for my dad's work, and every new school taught me to read a room fast: who gets heard, whose ideas get built on, and who goes quiet because they're not sure they belong. By the third school I stopped waiting to be included and started pulling other people in.

That instinct followed me into engineering. As a woman in the room, I kept noticing the same idea land differently depending on who said it, so I made it my job to shift that, not just notice it.

It's also where I learned how I like to solve problems. Leading supplemental instruction for an engineering course, I watched 64 students cram in before an exam and realized they could follow worked examples but froze on anything unfamiliar. They didn't need another example, they needed to recognize which method a problem called for. So I built decision-tree worksheets that taught the pattern instead of the answer: 21 of them over the semester, each revised from whatever tripped students up the week before. The ones that worked were the ones students could use without me, and the next SI leader reused them. That's still how I think about product: solve the problem people actually have, and build it so it scales past you.
