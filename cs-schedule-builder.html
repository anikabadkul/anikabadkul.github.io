PM Case Study · COMPSCI 325

Rebuilding Schedule Builder

Every semester, roughly 24,000 UMass undergrads open Schedule Builder to register for courses. The three things they need most, prerequisites, conflicts, and degree progress, aren't in it. I spent a semester finding out why, then redesigning what it should be.

01 · The Problem

Schedule Builder decides academic progress, but it was built for the system, not the student.

Miss a prerequisite, land in a conflict, or lose a seat, and the cost is real at 24,000 students a semester. The core problem: it forces students to hold too much in their heads. It won't say which course conflicts or why, won't show whether you've met prerequisites, and sends you to SPIRE for anything else.

So the goal wasn't a prettier UI. It was keeping students in one place instead of five tabs and a spreadsheet.

The brief

"I wish Schedule Builder would combine itself with the ARR so we know what courses we need."

02 · Research & Discovery

Students struggled for different reasons depending on how far into their degree they were.

We ran a 30-person survey and 9 interviews across a mix of class years. I wrote the research questions and analyzed the data, then used affinity mapping to cluster findings by user type. That clustering is what surfaced the clearest signal of the whole project.

04 · Approach & Decisions

With one semester, the first real PM decision was what to cut.

The data pointed at four friction points. Waitlist integration topped the survey (76% wanted it) but needed backend changes across two separate systems, so we scoped it out. The three features we kept were all solvable inside the existing interface, that constraint drove every decision after.

The fix I'm most proud of came later: my heuristic analysis caught that our conflict icons relied on red versus green alone, a colorblind failure none of us had flagged in design. It became one of the biggest changes between rounds.

Each feature below started as a hand-drawn wireframe and ended as a high-fidelity design. The sketch annotations were the spec; the screens are where they landed.

Feature 01

Inline Prerequisite Information

Prerequisite status shows inside the course listing, wired to the student's ARR, instead of forcing a trip to SPIRE. We kept the Add Course button active regardless of status, since overrides are common and gating it would lock out legitimate edge cases.

Feature 02

Integrated Academic Requirements (ARR) Tab

Degree requirements live as a tab inside the existing right-side panel, so students toggle between courses and requirements without leaving Add Course. A split-screen option handles users who need both at once.

Feature 03

Course Conflict Tracking

A conflict icon on every course (red exclamation, green check) with expandable inline detail, narrowing from course-level to section-level as you lock sections. Icons use shape and color together, so they work for colorblind users.

05 · Testing & Tradeoffs

I labeled the rounds by what each one changed, not by fidelity.

Three rounds of usability testing with heuristic analysis between each. The point isn't the prototypes; it's the decisions they forced.

🚩
Round 1 · Caught the accessibility failure

Hand-drawn wireframes, 4 students. "In Progress" labels confused people, and color-only conflict icons excluded colorblind users.

🛠️
Round 2 · Fixed what the flags found

Added icon shapes alongside color, plain-language labels, and tooltips on the expand button.

📋
Round 3 · The change I didn't see coming

A participant suggested reformatting prerequisites as a table, not a paragraph. I'd only questioned the content, never the format. It shipped, and was immediately more readable.

What we left out, and why

Waitlist integration was our highest-demand feature (76%). We cut it anyway, it needed backend changes across two systems, outside our scope. A PM who can't say no to good ideas is just as dangerous as one who ignores user needs.

06 · Outcome

Three fragmented tasks, consolidated into one interface.

The final prototype brings prerequisite checking, degree tracking, and conflict identification into one place. Students can answer "Can I take this? Have I met what I need? Why is this conflicting?" without leaving the platform. The best insight of the whole project came from a user, not the team, reformatting prerequisites as a table. That's the kind of thing you can't generate without real users.

If this shipped, what I'd measure

North star: the share of students who finish a registration session without leaving the platform. Secondary: task time for prerequisite checks, conflict resolution, and ARR lookup. Before rollout I'd run a two-week A/B test on the table format versus the original list. Measurement is how you know.

07 · What I Learned

Spotting a problem and deciding it's worth solving are different skills.

01
The problem you name shapes the solution you build

We started with "Schedule Builder is confusing" and reframed it as "it forces students to hold too much in their heads." That shift changed what we prioritized, and what we left out.

02
Visibility and readability are not the same thing

The prerequisite info was visible, but a user's idea to make it a table is what made it readable. You can build something technically correct that still fails the user.

03
Adapted users can mask a broken system

Seniors had built workarounds so smooth they barely noticed the friction. The real signal came from the freshman who just couldn't figure it out.

04
Accessibility is a constraint, not an afterthought

Our conflict icons relied on color alone, which fails colorblind users. Adding icon shapes cost almost nothing and should have been in scope from day one.

The takeaway

The gap between spotting a problem and deciding it's worth solving is where the real PM work lives.

"Designing for visibility isn't the same as designing for usability, and that gap is where the real PM work lives."
