Rebuilding Schedule Builder

Every semester, roughly 24,000 UMass undergrads open Schedule Builder to register for courses. Three things they need most — whether prerequisites are met, which courses are conflicting, and where they stand on degree requirements — aren't in it. I spent a semester finding out why, then designing what it should be instead.

01

The Problem

Every semester, nearly 24,000 undergraduates at UMass Amherst use Schedule Builder to register for courses — a tool that directly determines academic progress, degree completion, and in some cases, whether someone graduates on time at all. Get it wrong and you miss a requirement, land in a conflict, or lose a seat in a class you need. The interface was designed for the system, not the student — and our research showed that gap has real consequences at scale.

The core problem I kept coming back to in my analysis: Schedule Builder forces students to hold too much in their heads. It doesn't show which courses conflict or why. It doesn't surface whether you've met prerequisites. And if you want to join a waitlist or check your degree requirements, it sends you somewhere else entirely — back to SPIRE, into a separate tab, into a spreadsheet you built yourself.

When I looked at our interview data, this fragmentation landed differently depending on who you talked to. A freshman trying to understand what they're even allowed to take. A senior desperately cross-referencing their ARR with a spreadsheet they'd been updating for three years. Both doing the same task. Both failed by the same interface.

The Brief

"Design improvements to UMass Schedule Builder that reduce cognitive load, surface missing information, and eliminate the need to leave the platform to complete core scheduling tasks."

3 Friction points identified in initial audit
9 User interviews across experience levels
30 Survey respondents across all class years
3 Prototype iterations with usability testing
02

Research & Discovery

Our team ran a 30-person survey and 9 semi-structured interviews. I helped write the research questions and analyzed the data — then used affinity mapping to cluster interview findings by user type. That's what surfaced the freshman/senior split as the sharpest signal: two groups, failing for entirely different reasons.

What the interviews revealed
Freshmen
Confusion with basic interface structure
First-years conflated the shopping cart with the tentative schedule view. They relied on advisor-provided plans and couldn't tell what courses they were eligible to take without leaving the platform entirely.
Upperclassmen
Workaround fatigue
Seniors had built Excel spreadsheets, bookmarked the ARR in a separate tab, and used Coursicle for seat notifications. They'd adapted — but that adaptation was the signal. A good interface doesn't require workarounds.
Both groups
Conflict identification, not just detection
Every participant could see that a conflict existed. None could immediately see which course was causing it. That distinction — between knowing a problem exists and knowing how to fix it — became a core design target.

04

Approach & Decisions

The data pointed clearly at four friction points — but we had one semester, so the first real PM decision was what to cut. Waitlist integration was the highest-rated feature in our survey (76% wanted it) but it required backend changes across two separate systems. We scoped it out. The three features we kept — prerequisite display, ARR tab, conflict tracking — were all solvable within the existing interface without touching SPIRE's backend. That constraint shaped every design decision that followed.

I contributed to prototyping and documentation across all three features. The piece I'm most proud of: my heuristic analysis in M4 caught that our conflict icon system was an accessibility failure — relying entirely on red vs. green with no shape differentiation. That's a colorblind exclusion issue that none of us had flagged in design. It became one of the most substantive fixes between the medium and high-fidelity rounds.

Feature 01
Inline Prerequisite Information
Rather than forcing students to navigate to external SPIRE pages, prerequisite completion status is displayed directly within the course listing, wired to the student's ARR. Status labels (Completed, In Progress, Not Met) with color and icon indicators. One key design decision: the Add Course button stays available regardless of prerequisite status. We debated whether to gate it — but overrides are common, and restricting access would have locked out legitimate edge cases. Flexibility won.

Feature 03
Course Conflict Tracking System
A standardized conflict icon next to every course — red exclamation for conflict, green checkmark for clear — with expandable inline conflict details. The system first shows broad course-level conflicts, then narrows to section-level as you lock sections. Co-locating the conflict indicator next to the Sections button makes the relationship between problem and resolution visually obvious. Icons use both shape and color to address accessibility for color-blind users.

What we left out — and why

Waitlist integration was our highest-demand feature — 76% of surveyed students wanted it. We cut it anyway. It required cross-system backend changes between Schedule Builder and SPIRE that were outside our scope and control. The three features we shipped were deliberately chosen because they could all be solved within the existing interface. A PM who can't say no to good ideas is just as dangerous as one who ignores user needs.

05

How It Iterated

Three prototype rounds with usability testing and heuristic analysis between each. Every round surfaced something the design hadn't caught — usually around visual clarity, information density, and what users assumed about interactivity. My heuristic analysis flagged several of the major issues that drove the changes between rounds.

Paper prototypes
Initial sketches — proof of concept
Hand-drawn wireframes for each of the three features, tested with 4 UMass students. We discovered the prerequisite status labels were confusing — particularly "In Progress" — and that conflict icons relying only on color created accessibility issues for colorblind users. The ARR content felt overwhelming in its raw SPIRE form.
Medium-fidelity
Heuristic analysis — my flags drove key fixes
In my heuristic analysis I flagged that conflict icons relying only on red/green were a colorblind accessibility failure, that "In Progress" was too ambiguous as a prerequisite label, and that the ARR expand icon had no visible affordance. Each became a fix in the revised prototype — icon shapes added alongside color, plain-language labels, and hover tooltips on the expand button.
High-fidelity
Final prototype — table format for prerequisites
A late-stage usability participant suggested reformatting prerequisite information as a clean two-column table rather than a bulleted paragraph. This hadn't occurred to us — we'd been focused on visibility of content, not readability of format. The table was immediately clearer to scan. Conflict text was also bolded for stronger typography when expanded, after participants reported the conflict details were hard to notice.
06

Outcome

The final prototype consolidates three previously fragmented tasks — prerequisite checking, degree requirement tracking, and conflict identification — into a single interface. Students no longer have to leave the platform to answer the most basic questions: Can I take this? Have I met what I need? Why is this conflicting?

The most valuable moment came in round three, when a usability participant suggested reformatting the prerequisite list as a table instead of a paragraph. That's the kind of insight that's impossible to generate without real users — I hadn't questioned the format at all, only the content. It made it into the final build and was immediately more readable. The fact that it came from a user, not the team, is the whole point.

If I were taking this to a real product team, the next step would be a lightweight A/B test on the prerequisite table format vs. the original bullet list, with task completion rate and time-on-task as the primary metrics. The heuristic analysis tells us it should work better — but shipping without measurement is just a guess with nicer fonts.

North star metric: % of students who complete a full registration session without leaving the platform. Secondary: task completion time for prerequisite check, conflict resolution, and ARR lookup. I'd run a two-week A/B test on the prerequisite table format vs. the original bullet list before a full rollout. The heuristic analysis predicts it'll work — measurement is how you know for sure.

07

What I Learned

01
The problem you name shapes the solution you build
We started with "Schedule Builder is confusing." Through analysis I helped reframe it as "Schedule Builder forces students to hold too much in their heads." That shift changed what we prioritized designing for — and what we consciously left out.
02
Visibility and readability are not the same thing
The prerequisite feature was visible — users could find it. But a participant's suggestion to reformat it as a table made it actually readable. I learned that it's possible to design something technically correct that still fails the user.
03
Adapted users can mask a broken system
Seniors had built workarounds so effective they barely noticed the friction anymore. The real usability signal came from the freshman who just couldn't figure it out. Experienced users adapting is not the same as the interface working.
04
Accessibility is a design constraint, not an afterthought
My heuristic analysis caught that the conflict icon system relied entirely on color — which fails colorblind users. Adding icon shapes cost almost nothing in design effort and made the feature work for everyone. It should have been in scope from day one.

"Designing for visibility isn't the same as designing for usability — and that gap is where the real PM work lives."

This project taught me to ask a harder question than "can the user find it?" The better one is: can they actually use it, confidently, without help? Answering that second question is almost always more interesting — and more actionable.
